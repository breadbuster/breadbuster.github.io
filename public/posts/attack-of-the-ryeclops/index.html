WTF is rye?⌗

Almost all ancient forms of food production centered around shifting cultivation, a resource-based subsistence farming method where humans clear land (often forest) temporarily to grow crops for 2-3 seasons and then move on to new sites, leaving the earlier ground fallow for several years to regain its soil fertility. This way of life can be traced back to Africa and/or China, where the forerunners of Homo erectus had emerged some 5 million years ago. They went on to spread across the world, and arrived in northern Europe less than 1 million years ago. Shifting cultivation has influenced European culture for perhaps ~10,000 years, and is still practiced by millions of people in the tropics.

Grain production required shifting cultivation. The primary cultivated plant throughout the Viking Age was barley. Cultivated rye (Secale cereale) is believed to have originated from either S. montanum, a wild species found in southern Europe and nearby parts of Asia, or from S. anatolicum, a wild rye found in Syria, Armenia, Iran, Turkestan, and the Kirghis Steppe.

Rye first appears in the archaeological record about 13,000 years ago. At the beginning, it was found as a weed widely distributed in wheat and barley fields. It apparently had co-evolved with wheat and barley for thousands of years until its value as a crop was finally recognized. Rye began to be cultivated circa the 6th century, and this can be considered to be the most important change in agriculture in this period. Like barley and oats, rye flourishes in cold and wet climate.

Roman naturalist Pliny the Elder1 (AD 23-79) was dismissive of a grain that may have been rye, disparaging it as “a very poor food” that “only serves to avert starvation”. He said it was mixed with spelt “to modify its bitterness, and even then it is very disagreeable to the stomach”

As wheat production spread west and north to cold climates with short summers, rye likely traveled there as impurities in wheat (possibly as a result of Vavilovian mimicry).

No rye, no Europe⌗

Rye is a hardy substance: Unlike wheat, millet, and barley, rye can grow in poorer soils with less sun and at higher altitudes, and it can thrive through damp, drought and winterkill. In other words, rye is made to order for northern and eastern Europe. It grew well in the highly acidic ash leftovers from burning conifers when nothing else did.

Soon it became evident that this weed was outpacing wheat due to its high frost-resistance level, adaptability to various soil types, and ability to ripen over the short northern summer. Over the years, people began to cultivate rye instead, as a separate crop. This gave new agency to people in formerly unproductive regions, because one could suddenly produce an abundance of food that wasn’t possible before. Bread made from rye also lasts longer.

There is also a parallel explanation for why rye became so widespread in the North:

“It is possible that the spread of rye cultivation was accelerated by the climatic catastrophe of AD 536, as the latter may have caused both barley and wheat crops in fields to fail, while rye, as a less demanding cereal, at least produced seed grain. It may have been as a result of this that pure rye seed was first obtained over an extensive area.”

  • Andres Tvauri, The Migration Period, Pre-Viking Age, and Viking Age in Estonia

Rye fed Geoffrey Chaucer’s London and Fran√ßois Villon’s Paris, fueled the empires of Charlemagne and Peter the Great, and filled the ships of the Hanseatic League – the vast medieval confederation of merchant guilds and market towns that for ~500 years united the nations of the North and Baltic Seas and is widely seen as a precursor to the supranational model of the European Union. And it was rye that sustained the first Europeans as they settled in North America.

The OG Finnish rye⌗

The rapid growth of rye cultivation increased food supply and encouraged human migration to brutally cold and formerly inhospitable northern Europe.

The story of the Finnish rye is deeply intertwined with the history of Forest Finns, who originated in geopolitical border skirmishes since the 13th century.

Birger Jarl allegedly established Swedish rule in Finland through the Second Swedish Crusade, most often dated to 1249. The Novgorod Republic gained control of Karelia in 1278, the region inhabited by speakers of eastern Finnish dialects. Sweden, however, gained the control of western Karelia with the Third Swedish Crusade in 1293. Western Karelians were henceforth viewed as part of the western cultural sphere; eastern Karelians were culturally closer to Russia and the Eastern Orthodox Church and thus generally considered a separate people, even though they remain closely related to the Finns, both linguistically and ethnically. As a result, the northern part of the de facto border between Catholic and Orthodox Christendom became established at the eastern border of what would become Finland, with the Treaty of N√∂teborg between Sweden and Novgorod in 1323.

The powers to the east of Finland, namely Novgorod and later Russia, constantly challenged the Swedish sovereignty of the often sparsely populated areas of eastern Finland. To secure their realm, the Swedish kings encouraged farmers to settle these vast wilderness regions in eastern Finland, where the slash-and-burn agriculture was the dominant means of survival.

Living conditions in frontier lands were rough, aside from ongoing conflicts with the indigenous S√°mi and Karelian peoples. The cultivation of rye brought with it a rapid increase in population. The fact that slash-and-burn itself is a very land-intensive farming method that requires either a low human population density or a constant supply of new “frontier” lands also contributed to overpopulation in eastern Finland.

By late-16th century, the Swedish crown encouraged (some say “forced”) the Forest Finns from Savonia and Northern H√§me (Tavastland) to migrate westward and settle the wilderness in central and western Finland, in order to secure the vast border areas of the kingdom. The Forest Finns didn’t find many large forest areas there, so they continued on to present-day Sweden. Within a few decades, the Forest Finns had populated most of these sub-alpine Swedish forests.

From the 1620s on, the Forest Finns began settling in Norway. The largest concentration of settlements was in the eastern part of Hedmark, along the Norwegian-Swedish border. That area is denoted today as Finnskogen in Norwegian and Finnskog in Swedish, literally “the Finnish woods.” There, on those high-altitude forested ridges, the Forest Finns made a living by producing Finnish rye (called svedjerug in Norwegian, literally “slash-and-burn rye.)” In this rather remote forest belt, they were also able to move back and forth between the two countries, as the border itself was not properly established until 1751.

Finnforest culture has its roots to the east, and is found throughout northern Eurasia. Shifting cultivation was the main means for the survival of the forest Finns. Shifting cultivation was a very land-intensive farming method, resulting in a constant need for new areas. This was the reason for the migration, which spread slowly westward. They searched for new forests, needing spruce of good quality. Forest Finns came to Norway between 1620 and 1630. They first settled in the forests bordering Sweden, but then spread further into the eastern part of Telemark in the west. There are registered Forest Finns settlements in 40 municipalities in Hedmark, Oppland, Oslo, Akershus, √òstfold and Telemark counties. Norsk Skogfinsk Museum main task is to preserve and show the world the Forestfinns culture.

In Denmark-Norway (back then Norway was part of a union), the authorities in Copenhagen were allegedly in favor of the Forest Finns' immigration and their slash-and-burn agriculture due to the relatively high yields of rye production, compared to traditional Norwegian staples. These Forest Finns became known locally in Norway as Rugfinner (Rye Finns) or notably Svedjefinner (slash-and-burn Finns).

However, from the mid-17th century, the locals showed dissatisfaction with the newcomers, and the authorities issued new regulations to slow down Finnish immigration.

At the same time, a nascent iron industry was growing, where smelting was powered by charcoal. The Forest Finns' slash-and-burn agriculture was suddenly considered an economic threat to the iron industry because burning off now valuable timber increased the cost of charcoal. The state authorities officially banned the burning of forests in 1647, and the Finns were obliged to support the iron factories by providing charcoal at an artificially depressed price.

As often said, politics is downstream from culture, which is downstream from economics.

The cultivation of Finnish rye continued for another few hundred years, until the onset of World War I. Because this slash-and-burn technique involves the felling and burning of trees for temporary crop cultivation, it’s blamed for deforestation, soil erosion, loss of biodiversity, and climate change. The last burn was done in the 1930s in Finland. Today, carefully controlled slash-and-burn is practiced in local villages around Finland, in order to keep the heritage alive.

The rediscovery of rye⌗

Rye might have led to the Salem witch trials, as Linda Caporael argued in a 1976 article in Science magazine. Rye (eaten as a cereal and as a common ingredient of bread) can be infected with ergot, a fungus which invades developing kernels under warm and damp conditions – known to have existed at the time in Salem. “Convulsive ergotism causes violent fits, a crawling sensation on the skin, vomiting, choking, and–most interestingly – hallucinations. The hallucinogenic drug LSD is a derivative of ergot.”

Although rye is extraordinarily strong and easy to grow, many Scandinavian farmers abandoned it as a food crop and grew it mostly for animal feed and as a cover crop to plow nutrients back into the soil.

After World War I, the Finnish slash-and-burn rye was considered a lost variety – until 1973 when Per Martin Tvengsberg, a 37-year-old curator and historian at Hedmark Museum in eastern Norway, found 10 rye grains between the floorboards at an old grain-drying house in Grue Finnskog, the heart of “the Finnish woods.”

Tvengsberg then carefully planted these precious seeds to see if any would germinate on the off-chance. Well, 7 of these grains did sprout! Tvengsberg convinced the gardeners at the Hamar Domkirkeodden Urtehagen (herb garden at the Hamar Cathedral ruins) to grow these seedlings out. Thus began the salvation for the Finnish rye.

After several years, they had built up a decent supply of the rye grain. Eventually, the descendants of these 7 seeds made it into the Svalbard Global Seed Vault. Now this Finnish rye is grown all over the Nordic fields. The variety has been named “Svedjerug Tvengsberg,” after the man who rediscovered it. In Swedish literature, it’s most commonly called midsommarr√•g (“midsummer rye”), referring to the fact that the cultivar was traditionally sown in a burnt-off area in mid-summer. (The crop is typically lightly grazed or cut as green fodder for animals the same fall, and harvested the year after like a normal winter rye.) In English, it’s often called “Tvengsberg midsummer rye” – because “slash and burn” just doesn’t sound as poetically elegant or politically correct.

Thanks to decades of research, the grain has been rehabilitated through organic farming methods that make it possible to conserve its unique taste.

Generally, rye has a large number of kernels per plant. And this Finnish rye, oh boy, produces an extreme number that’s worthy of a Guinness world record all on its own! Under optimal conditions, Sw√§rd says, as many as 100 stalks can be produced from a single seed, and each of these stalks can yield <=100 kernels per seed-head – that’s a mesmerizing total of 10,000 grains from a single plant! What this means is, in a short amount of time, a farmer can increase their grain output exponentially, constrained only by the acreage available for planting. No wonder people used to say, if all you had was a pocket full of rye, you could survive.

Sw√§rd also explains why it’s important to not only store such heritage varieties as svedjerug in gene banks like Svalbard, but also continue to keep them alive by growing them and adapting them to changing conditions.

We don’t need a museum for conserving varieties. What we want is to grow them.
