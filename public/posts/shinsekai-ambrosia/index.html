Shinsekai no Ambrosia

Noma, the Copenhagen restaurant that helped define a genre of ultra high-end, locally foraged, and culinarily groundbreaking cuisine, is closing in 2024. Over the last decade, being able to experience Noma has apparently become a status symbol. Funny how the culinary icon was forced to shut down after it found itself facing financial difficulties.

In October 2021, Noma – arguably the most prestigious kitchen in the world – started paying its interns (known as stagiaires) for the first time. And that has added $50,000 to the restaurant’s monthly operating expenses, according to the NYT.

Rene Redzepi, chef-owner of Noma, said Monday that it wasn’t possible to make the math of fine dining work for his almost 100 employees and himself. … In 2021, the restaurant lost money, even with government support, while selling $700 lunches.

If Noma is fully booked every day, it would make at least $105k/week, >=$440k a month, or >= $5m a year. Numbers don’t lie.

I can’t bring myself to mourn Noma’s closure, because there is no part of me that feels something is being lost or taken away. And no, I’ve never dined at Noma, so I can’t tell you how life-changing a Noma meal was. I did read The Noma Guide to Fermentation a few years ago and tried making the rye bread miso (“ryeso”).

I’m glad Noma existed and Ren√© Redzepi realized a business that exploits unpaid labor is unsustainable, financially and morally. I hope the next restaurant that rises to influence global cuisine as much as Noma did will be a pioneer in some sort of real sustainability, not just propelled by fanaticism around a singular genius or its avant-garde fascism.

The truth is, however elaborately prepared or miraculously transformed, at the end of the day it’s just food – something that you swallow and inevitably becomes shit. Maybe that’s what made Anthony Bourdain so depressed.

A human being is primarily a bag for putting food into.

  • George Orwell

A lot of things are upside-down in the brave new Po-Lo (post-lockdown) world. Expensive fine dining as we know it seems to be on its way out. In fact, another destination for fine dining (in Silicon Valley), Manresa, closed in late 2022.

I am reminded of The Menu. The movie takes place mostly inside Hawthorne, a fictional uber-luxurious restaurant on a small island that seats only a dozen customers a night at $1,250/person. Workers are stuck between the belief that what they are doing (i.e. passionately pursuing their dreams) is worth sacrificing everything for and the reality that they have surrendered their lives to grueling service work which the monied class treats as merely social status accessories.

There is something strange about real life – you can feel so plugged in and so distant at the same time. You want to be part of the action, but you find yourself on the outside. And then, if you get close enough, you realize that the main goal of so many on the inside is to keep you out, and to make you think they know something that you do not, which is patently untrue.

Towards the end of The Menu, Chef Slowik pours his heart and soul into making Margot a good old-fashioned cheeseburger, because he sees it as an opportunity to redeem himself from his failure to stay true to his art and make up for all the passionless meals he prepared for the rich. When Margot takes a bite and appreciates the burger for what it is – a filling meal that satiates her hunger – the chef finally feels proud and content.

This burger reminds me of lablabi, a working-class staple in Tunisia. To make lablabi, you basically pour a thin soup with boiled chickpeas over a pile of stale bread that’s torn up into tiny pieces. Lablabi is cheap, quick, and filling; it fuels you for the rest of the day and keeps you warm during the winter.

At the other end of the dining spectrum is “trailer trash food”, stuff that I imagine the main character in Barbara Kingsolver’s Demon Copperhead grew up eating. I haven’t forgotten the shock I experienced when I first encountered the ambrosia at a working-class eatery in South Carolina: canned fruits were mixed with canned whipped cream, mayonnaise, sugar, and mini marshmallows, and topped with Cool Whip. It’s apparently a Southern holiday tradition. (The OG ambrosia made in the 1800s used only 3 ingredients: orange slices, grated coconut, and sugar.)

A friend of mine recently went to Italy on vacation and brought me a panforte di Siena – a traditional Italian confection that’s a cross between fruitcake, candy, and Lebkuchen. Basically, a dense, sweet “cake” made from candied fruits, nuts, spices, a touch of flour, and a sugar/honey syrup, dusted in powdered sugar on all sides. It tastes absolutely delicious, but far too sweet for me. I wanted to make a less sweet version with pretty much the same ingredients.

I stumbled across hutzelbrot, looked into it further, and fell down a rabbit hole… Eric Carle’s hungry caterpillar ate das Fr√ºchtebrot??

Hutzelbrot is a German fruit bread, originally made with dried pears (called “hutzeln” in Alemannisch), but it gained many regional names as well – “zelten” (Bolzano), “schnitzbrot” (Schwaben), “birnebrot” (Das Allg√§u), “kletzenbrot” (Bavaria-Austria) or simply the more generic “fr√ºchtebrot” as it can be filled any dried fruits other than pears.

In the old days, the stingy farmers' wives would dry the leftover pears to avoid food waste and make the hutzelbrot on St. Thomas' day, December 21st (the winter solstice). The wives and maids would embrace their pear trees, with their hands and arms still covered in dough, to ensure a good harvest in the following year.

Nothing beats ye olde traditional recipe. From Wikipedia:

Originally Fr√ºchtebrot was made without honey, brown sugar or white sugar, with the sweetness coming only from the dried pears. Equally uncommon is Fr√ºchtebrot made of wheat dough. Using wheat dough makes the bread look less rustic, but also hinders the combustion of the fruits baked into it.

So, it’s the medieval OG energy bar – just dried fruits, nuts and a touch of rye flour!!!

An 18th-century manuscript describes celteno, a predecessor of Zelten. The recipe reads: “Take some Candia grapes, raisins, pine nuts, peeled almonds cut long, minutely cut lemon peel, aniseed seeds, candied citron, cinnamon powder, a little sugar, and a little flour and mix everything together. Take a small piece of brewer’s yeast, the size of a walnut…”
