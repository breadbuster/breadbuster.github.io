Aquaboogie van Wheatsworth

2021-07-25 :: breadbuster
#pan de cristal  #heritage wheat  #turkey red  #triticale  #oberkulmer spelt  #whole wheat  #unsifted 

I have been experimenting with wholegrain pan de cristal. Impossible? “Water makes the baker rich.”

100% Turkey Red⌗

ingredient baker’s %
whole wheat flour (Turkey Red - Stephens Land & Cattle, KS) 100%
cold aqua (autolyse 75% + bassinage 45% + salt 10%) 130%
whole wheat levain 40%
salt 1%
olive oil (Peranzana) 20%

The process is pretty much the same as in the 80% extraction version – except that I gorged with dough a lot more water during bassinage and olive oil after.

A very light and airy crumb. Reminded me of my first Turkey Red bread, but this one is much more chewy and “spongy.”

70% Turkey Red + 30% triticale⌗

Triticale (Tricosecate) is a man-made cross of wheat (Triticum) and rye (Secale), exhibiting characteristics of both grains, i.e. triticale contains the complex flavor and nuance of rye, mellowed by the easier behavior of wheat. It has been around for well over a century. It was first bred in labs in the late 1800s in Scotland and Germany, in order to combine the yield potential and grain quality of wheat with the disease and environmental tolerance of rye.

In Star Trek episode “The Trouble with Tribbles”, the protection of a grain developed from triticale is the central focus. This grain was a genetically-engineered hybrid of wheat and rye with four distinct lobes per kernel, hence the name “quadro-triticale.” Mr. Spock attributes the ancestry of the root grain, triticale, to 20th-century Canada.

Indeed, in 1953 the University of Manitoba in western Canada began the first intensive triticale breeding program in North America, working mostly with durum wheat-rye crosses. Early efforts concentrated on developing a high-yield, drought-tolerant human food crop species suitable for marginal wheat-producing areas.

It was only after the 1970s that farmers started to grow this grain on a commercial scale – mostly for forage or fodder. Over the years, triticale’s popularity has surged and ebbed. Farmers tend to either love the crop and continually plant it, or give it a go once and decide they like other grains better. Triticale has recently resurfaced as a choice grain for high-protein feed.

In the vast potpourri of hybrid grains that have been developed so far, at least this one has been around for well over a century and has had more research than some other stuff that has been engineered in the last ~5 years.

Triticale is mostly grown in Europe (Poland, Germany, Belarus, France and Russia), but you can find it easily in North America and Australia these days.

In an attempt to better understand the idiosyncrasies of this grain, I ferreted out as much information as possible on the internet. What I’ve learned:

  1. Triticale actually has a higher protein content than wheat and, at the same time, a lower gluten content than wheat due to genetic inheritance from rye. The latter trait has deprived the grain of superstardom in the breadmaking fraternity. A suggested solution: blend <=30% triticale flour into a wheat dough.
  2. Unlike rye, triticale actually has a very poor ability to absorb water. Start with low hydration (~60%) if making a 100% triticale bread, and don’t expect a tall, fluffy loaf.
  3. Triticale appears to benefit from the LAB fermentation process of a natural leaven. This helps to make the protein more soluble and improves the viscosity of the dough itself.
  4. A triticale dough also benefits from minimal handling, i.e. “no knead” and “folding”, because it has a low mixing tolerance.

Triticale seems to be a good candidate for blending, rather than using it totally on its own.

ingredient baker’s %
whole wheat flour (Turkey Red - Stephens Land & Cattle, KS) 70%
whole triticale flour (Sunflower Acres, CO) 30%
cold aqua (autolyse 75% + bassinage 45% + salt 5%) 125%
whole triticale levain 40%
salt 1%
olive oil (Peranzana) 20%

The 70% wheat 30% triticale flour blend yielded a spongy bread with honeycomb crumb.

Compared to the 100% Turkey Red pan de cristal, this 30% triticale version has a milder flavor and a less open but more moist crumb. Tasty in a different way.

50% Turkey Red + 50% spelt⌗

ingredient baker’s %
whole wheat flour (Turkey Red - Stephens Land & Cattle, KS) 50%
whole spelt flour (Oberkülmer - Tom & Mary Klein, OH) 50%
cold aqua (autolyse 75% + bassinage 45% + salt 10%) 130%
caramelized onion purée 10%
whole spelt levain 40%
salt 1%
olive oil (from garlic confit) 20%
fermented jalapeños 15%
wild garlic leaves 5%
shredded cheese 20%

The jalapeños and garlic went in the dough during coil-folds.

I piled heaps and heaps of cheeses (from Hemme Brothers Creamery, MO) on top of the dough, just before it went into the oven:

  • aged cheddar
  • applewood smoked cheddar
  • sour cream & onion cheddar curds
  • garlic dill cheddar curds

Indulgent like those cheese slippers at Fervere Bakery in Kansas City.

The golden crispy cheese-laden crust is the crown jewel.

The crumb is juicy, bold and spicy.

After these experiments, I doubt I will miss making lower-hydration wheat breads again. If I’ve learned anything about my baking, it’s this: why “do” when I can “overdo”? The pan de cristal is a game-changer.

Only those who will risk going too far can possibly find out how far one can go.
